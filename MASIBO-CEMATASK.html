#Perfoming mean imputation on na values to remove them from the data frame.

# Function to apply mean imputation to numeric columns
fill_na_with_mean <- function(x) {
  if (is.numeric(x)) {
    mean_val <- mean(x, na.rm = TRUE)
    replace(x, is.na(x), mean_val)
  } else {
    x
  }
}
# Fill NA values with mean for numeric columns
data_imputed <- data %>% 
  mutate(across(where(is.numeric), fill_na_with_mean))

#Anova to check variability

let us do an anova test to check the statistical significance of the high variability seen above.

#Insights From the anova results, we reject the null hypothesis (which assumes no difference) and conclude that there are significant variations in acute malnutrition cases across different years in the dataset.

The analysis shows that there is a statistically significant difference in acute malnutrition cases among the different years from January 2021 to June 2023 in the dataset. The F-statistic of 33.76 and the very small p-value (7.7e-09) provide strong evidence to reject the null hypothesis, which assumes that there is no difference in acute malnutrition cases between the years. Given the upward treand, there is need for intervention to solve this prevalent issue in kenyan counties.

1.Descriptive Statistics: The descriptive statistics provide insights into the data’s characteristics. The dataset contains information on the total number of children dewormed, acute malnutrition cases, stunted and underweight children across Kenya’s counties from January 2021 to June 2023. There are some missing values in the ‘Acute Malnutrition’ column, totaling 355 values. The maximum number of children dewormed during the study period was 392,800. The average number of acute malnutrition cases per month was approximately 125.4, with some counties experiencing outliers, reporting as high as 4123.0 cases in a single month.

2.County-Level Variations: From the county-level analysis, Wajir had the highest number of acute malnutrition cases with an average of 1128.667, while Baringo had the lowest with an average of 16.59. The presence of significant variations among counties indicates a need for targeted health interventions in regions with higher prevalence rates of acute malnutrition.

3.Time Series Analysis: The time series analysis reveals an overall upward trend in acute malnutrition cases over time, particularly between 2021 and 2023. There were large fluctuations in 2022 and 2023, indicating high variability in acute malnutrition cases. This finding highlights the importance of understanding the underlying factors driving these fluctuations and addressing them through timely interventions.

4.Statistical Significance of Variability (ANOVA): The ANOVA results show significant variations in acute malnutrition cases across different years from January 2021 to June 2023. The rejection of the null hypothesis suggests that there is a statistical difference in acute malnutrition cases between the years, implying a need for targeted interventions based on year-specific trends.

6.Correlation Analysis: The correlation analysis shows weak to moderate positive correlations between the total number of children dewormed, acute malnutrition cases, diarrhoea cases, stunted 0-<6 months, and underweight 24-59 months. Conclusion and recommendations:

The data analysis reveals that acute malnutrition cases in children under 5 years exhibit an overall upward trend and significant variations among counties from January 2021 to June 2023.
